Who should apply

Curious about policy, not yet committed

We are looking for people in the AI safety pipeline who are early to mid-career, genuinely curious about policy, and not yet set on a specific path. A typical participant engaged seriously with AI safety during university, maybe through a student group, ML4Good, GCP or a research fellowship, and is now deciding what to do next.

AI safety context

You have engaged with the field beyond an online course and can reason about why frontier AI risks matter and what role policy plays in reducing them.

Genuine curiosity about policy

No policy experience needed. What matters is a sense of why finding this out now would be useful for you.

A working technical understanding

You can explain how modern AI systems are built and trained to a non-technical audience, roughly ML4Good or ARBOx level. Research experience is a plus, not a requirement.

Motivation and people skills

You want to reduce catastrophic risk from AI, and work that runs on conversations sounds at least a little exciting to you.

Questions

FAQ

What does it cost?

Nothing. Travel, housing, and the program itself are covered.

How technical do I need to be?

Enough to explain how AI systems are built and trained to a non-technical audience, roughly the level of an ML4Good bootcamp or ARBOx. You do not need research experience.

Do I need a policy background?

No. The first days cover the foundations, and the rest of the program is exactly about learning what these roles look like in practice.

I already know I want to work in policy. Should I apply?

Probably not, honestly. Fellowships like Horizon or TechCongress are built to place people who have already decided. This program is for people who have not.

What if I find out policy is not for me?

That is a fine outcome, and an intended one. You go back to technical or generalist work with a much better model of how DC thinks, how to communicate your work there, and people to call when policy questions reach you.

What happens after the two weeks?

The last two days are dedicated to next steps: career mapping, 1:1s, application planning, and a personal list of warm intros. If you decide to pursue policy, we will support you in taking the next concrete step.

How does the application work?

A short written application and one interview. Applications for the September cohort open soon, and if you express interest below we will email you as soon as they do.

Am I eligible if I am not a US citizen?

Many DC policy roles at think tanks and nonprofits do not require citizenship, while most congressional and government positions do. We will map what is open to you during the program.

Why is this called "Longitude"?

In 1714, the British Parliament passed the Longitude Act. Ships and crews were being lost because nobody could measure longitude at sea, a problem the government knew mattered but could not solve from within. The answer came from technical talent: John Harrison, a self-taught clockmaker, built a clock precise enough to keep London time on a rolling ship. AI policy is at a similar moment, and the hardest problems will not be solved without people who understand the technology in the room. The fine vertical lines across this site are lines of longitude.
