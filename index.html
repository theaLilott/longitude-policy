Who should apply

We are looking for impact-driven people in AI safety, early to mid-career, open to policy as their next step and ready to go for it if the two weeks show them where they fit.

AI safety context

You have engaged with AI safety and can reason about why frontier AI risks matter.

Motivation

You want to reduce catastrophic risk from AI and think that through policy work you could contribute to this.

FAQ

What does it cost?

Participation is free. We cover the program itself, housing in DC, meals on program days, and travel from within the US. If you join from abroad, we ask you to contribute to your flight, with the details agreed before you accept your spot.

How technical do I need to be?

Enough to explain how AI systems are built and trained to a non-technical audience, roughly what you would pick up in a technical AI safety bootcamp like ML4Good. Research experience is a plus but certainly not a requirement.

Do I need a policy background?

No. The first days cover the foundations, and the rest of the program is exactly about learning what these roles look like in practice.

I already know I want to work in policy. Should I apply?

It depends on how concrete your picture is. If you are already in a policy role or a fellowship like Horizon or TechCongress, this program will not add much for you. But if you are fairly sure you want policy and just do not know what the roles actually look like or how to get into them, the two weeks can give you exactly that overview and network.

What if I find out policy is not for me?

That is a great outcome too. You go back to technical or generalist work with a much better model of how DC thinks, how to communicate your work there, and people to call when policy questions reach you.

What happens after the two weeks?

The last day is dedicated to next steps: career mapping, 1:1s, application planning, and warm intros. If you decide to pursue policy, we will support you in taking the next concrete step.

How does the application work?

A short written application and one interview. Applications for the September cohort are open until Sunday, August 16, 2026, 11:59 PM PDT, and you can apply here.

How can I refer someone?

If you know someone who'd be a great fit for the Longitude DC Intensive, send us a referral through this short form. We follow up on every referral.

Am I eligible if I am not a US citizen?

Yes, as long as you are currently based in the US. Many DC policy roles at think tanks and nonprofits do not require citizenship, while most government positions do, and we will map what is open to you during the program. US citizens who are temporarily living abroad can also apply. What we cannot accept in this cohort are people who are neither US citizens nor based in the US.

Why is this called "Longitude"?

In 1714, the British Parliament passed the Longitude Act. Ships and crews were being lost because nobody could measure longitude at sea, a problem the government knew mattered but could not solve from within. The answer came from technical talent: John Harrison, a self-taught clockmaker, built a clock precise enough to keep London time on a rolling ship. AI policy is at a similar moment, and the hardest problems will not be solved without people who understand the technology in the room. The fine vertical lines across this site are lines of longitude. The red ones are the red lines AI development should not cross.
